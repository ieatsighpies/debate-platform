\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed in conversational and debate contexts,
yet their capacity to shift human beliefs and confidence in brief, turn-based interactions
remains underexplored. This thesis presents a pilot study examining whether debating against
a GPT-4o mini opponent — across three AI personality conditions (firm, balanced, open-minded)
— produces different belief or confidence change outcomes compared to human-human debate.
A custom debate platform was developed to collect within-subject attitude and confidence
measurements across debate rounds, with between-round reflections used to assess participant
engagement quality.

\section*{Key Findings}

Across a descriptive sample of $n = 90$ participants (model complete-case $n = 89$) in four debate conditions
(human-human control, firm AI, balanced AI, open-minded AI), primary and secondary analyses yielded consistent null results
with directional trends:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Null Belief Change}: AI debate produced no significant effect on participant
          belief change relative to human-human debate across four pre-specified confirmatory
          models (M1,M2,M4,M5: $\beta = 5.233$--$8.695$, all $p > 0.35$). The observed effect size
          ($d = 0.286$) suggests a small-to-medium practical difference that the current
          pilot sample ($n = 89$) was insufficiently powered to confirm (observed power $= 0.20$;
          required $n \approx 350$; 175 per group).
    \item \textbf{Directional Confidence Change}: AI debate showed a consistent directional
          increase in participant confidence change ($\beta = 5.2$--$5.8$ points) that did
          not reach statistical significance (conf-M1--M2: $p = 0.17$--$0.21$).
    \item \textbf{Feature Validation Is Mixed}: GPT-based argument quality aligned strongly
          with human ratings (Spearman $\rho = 0.797$; human-only ICC$(2,k)=0.834$), whereas
          GPT-based reflection quality showed weak rank alignment with human ratings
          (Spearman $\rho = -0.088$), indicating unequal measurement reliability across
          exploratory feature families.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Contributions}

Treated as a pilot investigation, this thesis contributes to the emerging literature on
AI-mediated persuasion by: (1) providing stable effect size estimates ($d = 0.286$)
to inform sample size planning for future full-scale studies; (2) demonstrating that
individual-level engagement measures remain important covariates when modeling belief outcomes;
and (3) establishing
a reusable debate platform with transparent GPT-assisted quality scoring,
including explicit human-validation diagnostics (reflection human-only ICC$(2,k)=0.902$;
argument human-only ICC$(2,k)=0.834$).
These findings offer implications for AI's role in argumentation training, education,
and human-AI interaction research.
