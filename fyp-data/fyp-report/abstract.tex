\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Large language models are increasingly used in argumentative settings, but their
persuasive impact in sustained, turn-based debate remains unclear. This thesis reports
a pilot study comparing human-human debates with three AI-opponent personalities
(firm, balanced, open-minded) on a custom platform that captured belief and confidence
updates across rounds, alongside between-round reflections and post-debate attribution
responses. The main finding is a directional confidence-belief dissociation pattern:
belief change showed no directional trend in AI conditions (contrasting with human-human
participants who showed belief decrease, M = -7.905), while participants showed a descriptive
trend toward confidence increases after AI debates; however, neither confidence-change nor
belief-change estimates were identified at pilot sample size because confirmatory confidence
intervals included zero for both outcomes.
Observed power for the primary belief contrast was 20\% (Cohen's d = 0.286, model n = 89 complete cases),
with the pooled AI group comprising n = 69 and the human-human group n = 21.
Methodologically,
the study contributes a reusable information-hiding debate pipeline, transparent
model-sequence reporting, and AI-assisted quality features with convergent validation checks to support future
research on AI-mediated persuasion in education and argumentation contexts. As a pilot,
the study provides effect-size estimates and design guidance for subsequent confirmatory work.
