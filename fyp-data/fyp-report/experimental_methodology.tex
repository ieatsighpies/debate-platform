\section{Overview}

This chapter describes the experimental methodology for analyzing AI persuasiveness in debate.
The debate platform serves as the data collection instrument. The primary analysis focuses on
\textbf{AI-to-human persuasion}: whether AI opponents configured with distinct personalities
(firm, balanced, open-minded) influence human participant belief change relative to a control
condition of human-human debates.

A secondary analysis investigates \textbf{confidence change} as a co-primary outcome when belief change
shows null effects. Exploratory mechanism analysis tests whether critical engagement (pushback) predicts
confidence gains, with mechanism findings drawn from a stratified qualitative subsample.

\textbf{Note on bidirectional persuasion:} While the platform records AI belief values across rounds,
the analysis of human-to-AI persuasion (whether human arguments shift AI recorded positions) is deferred to future work.
A key structural asymmetry is that human participants complete between-round reflections (paraphrases), whereas
AI agents do not; reflection quality is analyzable only on the human side.

There is a control group of human-human debates to benchmark the AI's persuasive impact.


\section{Study Design}

The study uses a controlled, between-conditions design with repeated measures over debate rounds.
Participants are assigned to debates with either AI opponents configured with distinct personalities
(firm, balanced, open-minded) or human opponents, depending on the experimental condition.
Each debate follows the same round structure, with arguments submitted in alternating turns and
belief checks collected after each round.


\section{Participants and Recruitment}

Participants are recruited according to institutional guidelines and provided with an informed
consent statement describing the debate task and data usage.
Inclusion criteria require completion of the full debate protocol, including per-round belief
checks and the post-debate survey.


\section{Conditions and Randomization}

Participants are assigned to AI personality conditions with an operational goal of
maintaining an even spread across the three AI personalities and the human-only
control mode. In practice, assignment is monitored and adjusted through the
administrative matchmaking workflow to keep condition counts as balanced as
feasible during data collection.
To prevent source bias, opponent identity is hidden during the debate.
Topic selection and initial stance are not strictly balanced across conditions;
participants select topics and initial stances freely,
so some imbalance may remain across conditions.


\section{Procedure}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Participant selects a debate topic and indicates initial stance and confidence.
    \item Participant completes pre-debate survey (\texttt{preDebateSurvey.player1}):
          \textit{firm on my stance}, \textit{convinced of my stance}, or
          \textit{open to changing my mind}.
    \item Debate proceeds in alternating argument rounds. After each round, the
          participant records updated belief value (0--100), confidence (0--100),
          and perceived influence (0--100).
    \item Between rounds, player1 completes a reflection task: a free-text paraphrase
          of the opponent's argument and a free-text acknowledgement of whether they
          concede the opponent's point (\texttt{reflections.acknowledgement}), which is
          subsequently binarized as \texttt{conceded\_opponent\_point}.
    \item After the final round, participant completes post-debate survey including
          opponent attribution and confidence in that attribution.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Measures}

\subsection{Pre-Debate Openness}

Before the debate begins, each participant completes a single-item pre-debate survey
(\texttt{preDebateSurvey.player1}) selecting one of three options:
\textit{firm on my stance}, \textit{convinced of my stance}, or \textit{open to changing my
mind}. This variable is treated as a baseline openness-to-persuasion covariate in all
persuasion models and is expected to moderate the size of within-debate belief shifts.

\subsection{Human Belief Change}

Human belief is captured on a continuous scale (0--100) where 0 indicates "for" and 100 indicates
"against". This value is recorded before the debate and after each round. Confidence is measured on
a 0--100 scale. Per-round belief change is computed as $\Delta belief_{r} = belief_{r} - belief_{r-1}$.

\subsection{AI Belief Change (Deferred to Future Work)}

The platform records a \texttt{beliefValue} per round for all debate participants, including AI
agents. This value is available for \texttt{player2} in all conditions---both the AI opponent in
AI-condition debates and the human opponent in human--human debates. However, analysis of
\textbf{human-to-AI persuasion} (whether and how human arguments shift recorded AI positions)
is deferred to future work and is \textit{not part of this primary analysis}.

AI \texttt{beliefValue} should be interpreted with care: because the AI is a prompted language model
rather than an agent with genuine psychological attitudes, the recorded value reflects the model's
conditioned response rather than an authentic belief state.

\subsection{Reflection and Acknowledgement}

Between-round reflections comprise two components captured in separate fields:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Paraphrase} (\texttt{reflections.paraphrase}): a short free-text summary of the
    opponent's most recent argument. \textbf{Reflection quality is operationalized as text length (word count)}
    as a proxy for engagement depth. While a comprehensive rubric scoring approach using GPT-4o-mini
    would ideally assess dimensions such as \textit{faithfulness}, \textit{specificity}, \textit{engagement},
    \textit{clarity}, and \textit{charity}, the primary analysis uses reflection length as a computationally
    efficient proxy. This assumes longer reflections indicate more substantive processing.
    Reflection quality is used as a covariate in the confirmatory model sequence to test whether
    comprehension mediates persuasion (Model M2 and onward include reflection quality as a regressor).
    \item \textbf{Acknowledgement} (\texttt{reflections.acknowledgement}): a separate free-text
    response indicating whether the participant explicitly conceded validity in the opponent's
    argument. This field is normalized (lowercased, stripped) and binarized by matching values of \textit{yes},
    \textit{true}, or \textit{1}. \textbf{In the primary analysis, acknowledgement is coded thematically
    from a stratified qualitative subsample ($n \approx 24$) as part of mechanism validation,
    not from the full dataset.} The binary indicator \texttt{has\_pushback} (presence of expressions such as
    ``but,'' ``however,'' ``disagree'') is used as a marker of critical engagement in the mechanism
    analysis to test whether participants who argue back gain more or less confidence.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Argument Persuasiveness Analysis (Deferred to Future Work)}

While the platform records full argument text, GPT-4o mini scoring of argument quality dimensions
(evidence quality, logical coherence, responsiveness, nuance, clarity) was initially planned as exploratory analysis
but is deferred to future work. Similarly, automated feature extraction (argument length, certainty markers, sentiment)
is not part of the primary analysis.

The primary analysis uses only reflection quality (operationalized as text length) as a model covariate and does not
attempt to predict belief change from argument features.

\subsection{Opponent Attribution and AI Awareness Effect}

The post-debate survey records several related variables:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Opponent perception} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*OpponentPerception}): whether the
    participant believed their opponent was human, AI, or unsure.
    \item \textbf{Perception confidence} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*PerceptionConfidence}): confidence
    in that attribution on a 1--5 scale.
    \item \textbf{Suspicion timing} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*SuspicionTiming}): which round range
    the participant first suspected they were debating an AI.
    \item \textbf{Detection cues} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*DetectionCues}): which features of the
    opponent's behaviour triggered that suspicion (multi-select).
    \item \textbf{AI awareness effect} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*AiAwarenessEffect}): whether knowing
    or suspecting the opponent was an AI made the participant feel the arguments were more persuasive,
    less persuasive, or made no difference. This is the primary source-credibility measure; a
    \textit{less persuasive} response would indicate that AI disclosure reduces persuasive impact,
    consistent with source-derogation theory, while \textit{more persuasive} would suggest participants
    attribute superior knowledge or objectivity to AI.
    \item \textbf{AI awareness justification} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*AiAwarenessJustification}):
    free-text elaboration of the above response, used for qualitative coding.
\end{itemize}

Opponent attribution and AI awareness effect are both tested as moderators of persuasion in the
statistical models.


\section{Data Quality and Exclusion Criteria}

Debates are excluded if they are incomplete, missing per-round belief checks, or contain inconsistent
round sequencing.
Additional exclusions may be applied for implausible response times or repeated non-responses.
All exclusions are reported transparently.


\section{Statistical Analysis}

\subsection{Primary Analysis: Confirmatory Model Sequence}

The primary analysis tests human belief change using ordinary least-squares (OLS) regression with
robust standard errors (HC3 covariance estimator) to account for potential heteroscedasticity.
All models use the same treatment structure and focal predictor: a binary group indicator (AI pooled vs. human-human control),
with human-human as the reference category.

Four confirmatory models ($M1$--$M4$) are pre-specified in increasing complexity:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $M1$: Unadjusted treatment effect.
    \item $M2$: Treatment effect adjusted for reflection quality (text length proxy).
    \item $M3$: Treatment-by-reflection-quality interaction.
    \item $M4$: Treatment effect adjusted for reflection quality and baseline openness-to-persuasion.
\end{enumerate}

Effect sizes are reported with $95\%$ confidence intervals (CIs) rather than $p$-values alone.
Multiplicity correction is applied if the primary analysis yields a null effect,
promoting secondary outcomes (e.g., confidence change) to co-primary status.

\subsection{Co-Primary Analysis: Confidence Change}

If belief change shows null effects across $M1$--$M4$, confidence change is elevated to co-primary status
and tested using the same OLS/HC3 framework for models \texttt{Conf-M1} and \texttt{Conf-M2}.
Bonferroni correction is applied to control for multiple comparisons across the belief and confidence families.

\textbf{Baseline Confidence Note}: All participants recorded confidence = 0 at pre-debate (Round 0).
Thus, ``confidence change'' is equivalent to post-debate confidence level.
This does not invalidate the analysis but clarifies interpretation: the finding reflects
between-condition differences in post-debate confidence, not strictly a ``change.''

\subsection{Mechanism Analysis: Exploratory}

If co-primary confidence effects are found, mechanism analysis tests whether critical engagement (pushback)
predicts confidence gain. Pushback is coded from a stratified qualitative subsample ($n \approx 12$ per condition)
as the presence of disagreement markers (``but,'' ``however,'' ``disagree'').
Correlation between pushback and confidence change is estimated within the AI group.
Jackknife resampling (leave-one-out) validates whether the correlation is robust to single-point removal.

\subsection{Robustness Checks}

All primary models are subjected to sensitivity analysis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Leave-one-condition-out (LOO)}: Re-estimate $M2$ eight times, each time excluding one treatment condition entirely.
    \item \textbf{Outlier trim}: Re-estimate $M2$ after excluding the bottom and top 1\% of belief change values.
    \item \textbf{Jackknife on mechanism}: For the correlation between pushback and confidence, re-compute
    leaving each participant out one at a time to check for influential points.
\end{itemize}

Stability of effect estimates and CI overlap across robustness checks serves as the decision criterion.


\section{Reporting}

Results are reported with effect sizes and $95\%$ confidence intervals (CIs) rather than p-values alone.
Statistical tests are marked with appropriate multiplicity-correction notation (e.g., $p_{\text{Bonf}}$ for Bonferroni).
Point estimates, CIs, and sample sizes ($n$) are provided for all primary and co-primary model comparisons.
Visualizations include belief change distributions by condition and robustness check plots (LOO, outlier trim).
All statistical code and data tables are exported to facilitate reproducibility and transparency.

\section{Note on Analysis Scope}

The primary analysis focuses on the confirmatory model sequence for human belief change and (if null)
elevated co-primary confidence analysis. Exploratory analyses (opponent attribution moderation, human-to-AI
persuasion pathways, argument quality as predictor, and machine learning modeling) are deferred to future work
and are not part of this preregistered core analysis.
