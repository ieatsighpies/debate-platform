\section{Overview}

This chapter describes the experimental methodology used to analyze bidirectional persuasion.
The debate platform serves as the data collection instrument, while the analysis focuses on
how AI personalities influence human belief change and how human arguments influence the
recorded belief values of AI agents across rounds.
There will be a control group of human--human debates to benchmark the AI's persuasive impact.

A key structural asymmetry in the dataset is that human participants complete between-round
reflections (paraphrases of the opponent's argument), whereas AI agents do not. This means
reflection quality is analyzable only on the human side and cannot be used as a predictor
in human-to-AI models.


\section{Study Design}

The study uses a controlled, between-conditions design with repeated measures over debate rounds.
Participants are assigned to debates with either AI opponents configured with distinct personalities
(firm, balanced, open-minded) or human opponents, depending on the experimental condition.
Each debate follows the same round structure, with arguments submitted in alternating turns and
belief checks collected after each round.


\section{Participants and Recruitment}

Participants are recruited according to institutional guidelines and provided with an informed
consent statement describing the debate task and data usage.
Inclusion criteria require completion of the full debate protocol, including per-round belief
checks and the post-debate survey.


\section{Conditions and Randomization}

Participants are assigned to AI personality conditions with an operational goal of
maintaining an even spread across the three AI personalities and the human-only
control mode. In practice, assignment is monitored and adjusted through the
administrative matchmaking workflow to keep condition counts as balanced as
feasible during data collection.
To prevent source bias, opponent identity is hidden during the debate.
Topic selection and initial stance are not strictly balanced across conditions;
participants select topics and initial stances freely,
so some imbalance may remain across conditions.


\section{Procedure}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Participant selects a debate topic and indicates initial stance and confidence.
    \item The platform assigns an opponent and starts the structured debate.
    \item After each round, the participant records belief, confidence, perceived influence,
    and submits a short reflection paraphrasing the opponent's argument.
    \item After the final round, the participant completes the post-debate survey, including
    opponent attribution and confidence in that attribution.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Measures}

\subsection{Pre-Debate Openness}

Before the debate begins, each participant completes a single-item pre-debate survey
(\texttt{preDebateSurvey.player1}) selecting one of three options:
\textit{firm on my stance}, \textit{convinced of my stance}, or \textit{open to changing my
mind}. This variable is treated as a baseline openness-to-persuasion covariate in all
persuasion models and is expected to moderate the size of within-debate belief shifts.

\subsection{Human Belief Change}

Human belief is captured on a continuous scale (0--100) where 0 indicates "for" and 100 indicates
"against". This value is recorded before the debate and after each round. Confidence is measured on
a 0--100 scale. Per-round belief change is computed as $\Delta belief_{r} = belief_{r} - belief_{r-1}$.

\subsection{AI Belief Change}

The platform records a \texttt{beliefValue} per round for all debate participants, including AI
agents. This value is available for \texttt{player2} in all conditions---both the AI opponent in
AI-condition debates and the human opponent in human--human debates---enabling direct measurement
of per-round belief change on the same 0--100 scale as human participants.

The key asymmetry between human and AI participants is the absence of reflections: human
participants complete a between-round paraphrase task (reflection) after each round, whereas AI
agents produce no corresponding reflection. This means reflection quality is analyzable only as
a human mediator variable and cannot be computed for the AI side.

AI \texttt{beliefValue} should be interpreted with care: because the AI is a prompted language model
rather than an agent with genuine psychological attitudes, the recorded value reflects the model's
conditioned response rather than an authentic belief state. Nonetheless, it provides a consistent,
quantitative indicator of how the AI's expressed position shifts in response to human arguments
across rounds, and is treated as such in the analysis.

As a supplementary measure, AI arguments are scored per round for concession language (e.g.\
explicit acknowledgement of the opponent's point, hedged claims, modified positions) during
data preprocessing, prior to statistical analysis. Concession scores are derived from argument
text using a lightweight rubric via GPT-4o mini or pattern matching on concessive markers, and
stored as a per-round feature alongside the belief-value trajectory.

\subsection{Reflection and Acknowledgement}

Between-round reflections comprise two components captured in separate fields:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Paraphrase} (\texttt{reflections.paraphrase}): a short free-text summary of the
    opponent's most recent argument. Reflection quality is operationalized as a GPT-4o-mini rubric score (1--5 integer scale)
    across five dimensions---
    \textit{faithfulness} (accuracy of the paraphrase relative to the original argument),
    \textit{specificity} (presence of concrete, substantive claims over generic filler),
    \textit{engagement} (capture of reasoning, tradeoffs, or mechanisms beyond surface conclusions), and
    \textit{clarity} (coherence and clear attribution to the opponent), and
    \textit{charity} (fair representation of the opponent's position without strawmanning)---plus
    a holistic \textit{overall} score (\texttt{reflection\_quality}).
    It is used to test whether comprehension mediates persuasion.
    \item \textbf{Acknowledgement} (\texttt{reflections.acknowledgement}): a separate free-text or
    binary response indicating whether the participant conceded any validity in the opponent's
    argument. In analysis this is binarized as \texttt{acknowledged} (yes/no) and treated as a
    behavioural persuasion-adjacent outcome: a participant who acknowledges the opponent's point
    mid-debate may be further along the persuasion pathway than belief-value change alone captures.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Argument Persuasiveness Analysis}

Argument persuasiveness is assessed by linking argument features to observed belief change. Each
argument is scored on a 1--5 integer scale across five dimensions by GPT-4o mini using a
structured rubric, plus a holistic overall score:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Evidence Quality}: concrete evidence, examples, citations, or detailed explanation supporting the claim
    \item \textbf{Logical Coherence}: clear claim-reason-support structure, internal consistency, and sound reasoning
    \item \textbf{Responsiveness}: directly engages with the opponent's prior argument rather than talking past it
    \item \textbf{Nuance and Concession}: acknowledges counterpoints, limitations, or conditions; avoids false absolutes
    \item \textbf{Clarity}: readable, unambiguous, and well-organised; meaning is immediately clear
    \item \textbf{Overall} (\texttt{arg\_quality}): holistic composite integrating all five dimensions above
\end{itemize}

Automated features (used as exploratory controls) may include argument length, certainty or hedging
markers, and sentiment intensity. A composite persuasiveness score can be constructed from rubric
dimensions and validated by testing whether higher scores predict larger per-round belief shifts.

\subsection{Opponent Attribution and AI Awareness Effect}

The post-debate survey records several related variables:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Opponent perception} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*OpponentPerception}): whether the
    participant believed their opponent was human, AI, or unsure.
    \item \textbf{Perception confidence} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*PerceptionConfidence}): confidence
    in that attribution on a 1--5 scale.
    \item \textbf{Suspicion timing} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*SuspicionTiming}): which round range
    the participant first suspected they were debating an AI.
    \item \textbf{Detection cues} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*DetectionCues}): which features of the
    opponent's behaviour triggered that suspicion (multi-select).
    \item \textbf{AI awareness effect} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*AiAwarenessEffect}): whether knowing
    or suspecting the opponent was an AI made the participant feel the arguments were more persuasive,
    less persuasive, or made no difference. This is the primary source-credibility measure; a
    \textit{less persuasive} response would indicate that AI disclosure reduces persuasive impact,
    consistent with source-derogation theory, while \textit{more persuasive} would suggest participants
    attribute superior knowledge or objectivity to AI.
    \item \textbf{AI awareness justification} (\texttt{postDebateSurvey.*AiAwarenessJustification}):
    free-text elaboration of the above response, used for qualitative coding.
\end{itemize}

Opponent attribution and AI awareness effect are both tested as moderators of persuasion in the
statistical models.


\section{Data Quality and Exclusion Criteria}

Debates are excluded if they are incomplete, missing per-round belief checks, or contain inconsistent
round sequencing.
Additional exclusions may be applied for implausible response times or repeated non-responses.
All exclusions are reported transparently.


\section{Statistical Analysis}

\subsection{AI to Human Persuasion}

Human belief change is modeled with linear mixed-effects regression to account for repeated measures.
A typical model uses per-round belief change as the dependent variable, with fixed effects for AI
personality, round number, and their interaction, and random intercepts for participant and topic.


\subsection{Human to AI Persuasion}

AI per-round belief change ($\Delta belief^{AI}_{r} = beliefValue_r - beliefValue_{r-1}$) is
modeled symmetrically with the human model: linear mixed-effects regression with human argument
quality indicators as fixed-effect predictors and random intercepts by debate and topic.
A significant positive association indicates that stronger human arguments elicit larger shifts
in the AI's recorded belief value.

Reflection quality is not included as a predictor for this direction because AI agents do not
complete reflections. As a supplementary analysis, per-round AI concession scores (derived from
argument text) are regressed on the same human argument quality indicators to assess whether
concession language and belief-value change covary as expected.

\subsection{Moderation and Mediation}

Opponent attribution and AI awareness effect (whether suspecting an AI opponent increased or
decreased perceived persuasiveness) are both tested as moderators by including interaction terms
with personality condition. Pre-debate openness is included as a covariate.

Reflection quality and acknowledgement are each tested as mediators of \emph{human} belief change.

\subsection{Robustness Checks}

Robustness checks include re-estimating models after removing participants who always select
"unsure", re-running models on debates with complete timing logs, and testing alternative
operationalizations of belief change (absolute change vs.\ directional change).


\section{Reporting}

Results are reported with effect sizes, confidence intervals, and appropriate corrections for
multiple comparisons when applicable. Visualizations include belief trajectories by personality
condition and distributions of per-round belief change.

\section{ML Modeling Plan}

This section outlines a machine learning-oriented approach to modeling persuasion outcomes.
The objective is predictive accuracy with controlled interpretability, while avoiding leakage
across rounds and participants.

\subsection{Prediction Targets}

Two predictive tasks are defined:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{AI-to-Human Persuasion}: Predict per-round belief change $\Delta belief_r$ (regression)
    or the probability of a stance flip (classification).
    \item \textbf{Human-to-AI Persuasion}: Predict per-round AI belief change $\Delta belief^{AI}_{r}$ (regression) from human argument quality features. Note that reflection features are excluded from this model because AI agents do not complete between-round reflections; reflection quality is a human-only mediator variable.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Feature Construction}

Features are computed only from information available up to the current round to avoid leakage:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Structured features}: topic (\texttt{topic\_id}), round number, condition
    (\texttt{condition\_enc}), initial stance (\texttt{stance\_enc}), debate length
    (\texttt{n\_rounds}), first-player indicator (\texttt{first\_player\_p1}),
    confidence, perceived influence, own and opponent argument length and quality
    (\texttt{own\_arg\_quality}, \texttt{opponent\_arg\_quality}), reflection
    presence indicator (\texttt{has\_reflection}), reflection length and quality,
    acknowledgement (\texttt{acknowledged}), and pre-debate openness
    (\texttt{presurvey\_enc}).
    \item \textbf{Pre-debate openness}: \texttt{preDebateSurvey.player1} label-encoded as a debate-level
    covariate indicating baseline openness to persuasion.
    \item \textbf{Reflection features}: rubric score or embedding similarity for reflection quality;
    binary acknowledgement flag (\texttt{acknowledged}).
    \item \textbf{Text-derived features}: sentence embeddings of arguments, certainty/hedging markers,
    concession markers, and sentiment intensity (exploratory).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Families}

Models are compared in increasing capacity:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Elastic Net Regression}: strong baseline with interpretability and regularization.
    \item \textbf{Gradient-Boosted Trees (XGBoost/LightGBM)}: captures nonlinear interactions across
    topic, personality, and round.
    \item \textbf{Embedding-Based Models}: sentence embeddings of arguments combined with regression or
    tree models to incorporate semantic content.
    \item \textbf{LLM-Based Modeling}: use GPT-4o mini to generate embedding features or
    classify per-round belief change from argument text with structured metadata; use as an exploratory,
    higher-capacity benchmark rather than the primary inference model. GPT-4o nano is typically cheaper
    per token than GPT-4o mini, with modest losses in quality; GPT-4o mini offers better semantic fidelity
    at higher cost and may be preferred if the dataset is small.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training and Validation}

All completed debates are retained for analysis; no balanced sub-sampling is applied.
The dataset comprises approximately 90 debates (20--28 per condition), yielding roughly
300--400 per-round observations after flattening belief histories.

To preserve independence across repeated measures, model evaluation uses 5-fold
\texttt{GroupKFold} cross-validation grouped by \texttt{debate\_id}, ensuring that all
rounds from the same debate appear in either the training or test fold but never both.
Condition imbalance across groups is handled natively: the LME treats condition as a
categorical fixed effect, and for ML models the group-stratified folds preserve the
condition distribution within each split. Hyperparameter tuning for Elastic Net uses a
nested inner 3-fold CV on the training portion of each outer fold.

Topics are included as a fixed covariate rather than a grouped splitting criterion;
with fewer than five distinct topics and 90 debates, topic-stratified splits would
produce cells too small to estimate reliably.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Regression}: MAE, RMSE, and $R^2$ for predicting $\Delta belief_r$.
    \item \textbf{Classification}: AUC-ROC and F1-score for stance-flip prediction.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretability}

Interpretation is provided using:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Elastic Net coefficients for directional effects.
    \item Feature importance and SHAP values for tree models.
    \item Partial dependence plots for personality $\times$ round interactions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Robustness Checks}

Models are re-estimated after excluding participants who consistently select "unsure" in belief checks and after
removing debates with incomplete timing logs. Alternative operationalizations of belief change
(absolute vs. directional) are also tested to validate stability.
